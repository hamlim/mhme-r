Blog Post 4

(14/5/14)

About Snapchat, Improving my Workflow, and notifications

Snapchat

Snapchat is an amazing service, and I want to point out first before I continue that the idea I will lay out below originated before the release of the new features in the application. A few weeks ago I was on a snapchat run, communicating almost entirely with my friends with Snapchat. We routinely used Facebook messenger to communicate but now snapchat had become the norm to text friends. There was one clear feature that was missing in the experience however, and that feature still does not exist in today's application. This feature is the idea of groups. In almost any sort of web application that is intended as a social network there needs to be the idea/generalization of a group or team. It is simply natural to include a grouping function in a social network simply because that is what we do in real life, we group together to communicate. I still feel that this feature would make Snapchat even more friendly to people that still use services like Facebook messenger and others.

Workflow

When I began re-working my personal web page several weeks ago now, I had a pretty weak workflow that was broken up by unspecified amounts of time spent doing things that weren't work. I would continuously go to youtube to watch a few videos and other sites to try to get some news or whatever. I would then spend about 90% of my time on these other tasks and barely get any work done. The central problem was the lack of any well worked to do list. And I find that even to this day that to do lists must be the center or heart of a good workflow for anyone.

So I have been using Trello to handle all my to do lists, and as I tackle many projects at once it has proven to be quite a good task managing software to use, If you don't use it now, I highly suggest you give it a try. I also started using Draft as a default writing platform. it has some of the best markdown support I have found anywhere from using my code editors to using other services Draft is the one I keep coming back to. So that has been a little bit about my workflow, the next thing I want to talk/write about is notifications.

Notifications

With the recent news about Android wear and the Moto 360 as well as the LG G watch I wanted to discuss what I feel is the most important thing to tackle in the next few months for any developer, and I plan on putting it on the horizon to try to develop a unique solution to. The problem people should be focusing in on is creating a single cross-platform actionable syncing notification hub. Some other services provide minimal implementations of this idea in many products but they seem to always lack any actionable tasks. I think the next big thing is that web app developers will see a fewer amount of user visits and a larger amount of API calls. This is great for many reasons, and there really is only one way to prevent that from happening, which is to design a platform people will love. Ultimately I think it can be a good thing to develop an app that simply congregates all your notifications in one simple spot. I also plan on blogging about this a bit more in the future.

Blog Post 3

Written at 2:09 am EST Thursday March 13th by Matt Hamlin

Some more ideas about sharing, trends with searches and the next big thing for startups.

Throughout the week I have been working on my next big project, I have the first few steps completed, yet true to many other pieces about developing startups the product I am developing does not scale at all yet. One of the troubles of using twitter to develop big data trends is that the limitations of the twitter API prevents any large amount of data being read into an application. This development has been eye opening to me to realize just how much data is being requested by each user for an application of this size. I will work on developing a better backend for the site once I have finished with some of my silly school work.

The next thing I want to discuss/write about is the problem that my friends and I have encountered multiple times with classic search engines. Search engines like Google and Bing store data in ways that computers can understand but humans have to learn a lot in order to properly intake the information. The next thing humans tend to go to after Google doesn't return information they want is to turn to their friends either through social networks or in person to ask questions. I feel that startups like Quora are only the beginnning of the new age of search engines. Also I want to remind everyone that back in the early 2000's or so there was a startup called Qwiki if I remember correctly that wanted to solve the problem the other way around by translating what Google would provide in a search result into something that would be easier for humans to read and intake. I find it so interesting that two different groups of visionaries can see the same problem and attempt to tackle it differently.

Finally the last thing I wanted to quickly comment upon was the trend of curration in startups.I believe there are four levels of curration in any startup scene, the first of which is aggregation, the second is curation provided by friends, the third is curration through the form of a team of content editors, and finally the fourth element of curration is using computer algorithms and things like "Big Data" to help currate information and resources for users. These are the 4 levels of curration, as of now I can't comeent on which is the best form of curration and which isn't, but I can say that what Facebook does is a mash up of the fourth version and the second version of curration. For Facebook paper, the company has a team of content editors that fill the content in from their own resources. I think that whatever comes in the near future that is as big as Twitter, Facebook, Foursquare will have to take a stance for what type of curration they believe to be the one that will take us into the future.
